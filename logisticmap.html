3. Logistic Map

Logistic map is a one-dimensional (1D) discrete dynamical systems written by \[ x_{n+1} = ax_n(1-x_n), \] where $x_n$ is a steppng point on a line bitween 0 and 1.
$\textit{For example}$, this system represents the population per period when the reproductive rate is non-linear.
The stepping point travels to the next place at $x_{n+1}$ after the $n$ counts up.
Here, we can see the interesting trajectory of $x_n$ when the parameter of $a$ changes.
These trajectory in the below gif are in the two case of $x_0=0.40$ and 0.41.

The figure on the right shows the dependence of $x_{n+1}$ on $n$ steps. The complex trajectory in the range of $3.56 < a < 4.00$ is called as "Chaos".
Chaos is a behavior in which the trajectory changes significantly with small differences in initial values.
In the case of logistic map, the chaos is caused by the "discreteness" of the system.
If this system is continuous for time, we should replace the above relation by \[ \frac{d}{dt} x = ( a - 1 ) x ( 1 - \frac{a}{a-1}x ). \] This equation can be solved analytically as follows: \[ x(t) = \frac{(a-1)x(0)}{ax(0)(1-\rm{e}^{-(a-1)t}) + (a-1) \rm{e}^{-(a-1)t}}. \] This solution shows that $x(t)$ converges to 0 or $(a-1)/a$ at $t \rightarrow \infty$, even starting from any initial value of $x(0)$.
Thus, the continuous logistic map does not appear the chaos behavior.

Let's go back to the discrete one again and see the detail in the case of $x_0=0.40$ and 0.41.
At first, in range of $0< a < 1$, the $x_n$ converges to 0, as shown in this figure($a=0.8$).

Also, when $1 < a < 2 $, the $x_n$ converges to $(a-1)/a$ (figure: $a=1.26$).

These limitation can be calculated by the steady equation as follows: \[ x_n = ax_n (1-x_n). \] This is a quadratic equation that can be solved algebraically.
The solutions are 0 and $(a-1)/a$.
We can understand that the $x_n$ converges to the steady-solution of it.
Hereinafter we call the solution $x^*$.

However, when the $a$ becomes larger than 2, $x_n$ starts oscillating around the $x^*$.
The oscillation is caused by the negative growth rate, where the magnitude relation between $x_{n}$ and $x_{n+1}$ changes step by step around the $x^*$.
In the range of $2 < a < 3$, the $x_n$ still converges to $x^*$(figure: $a=2.94$).

And, as the $a$ increases, The trajectory gradually become difficult to close to $x^*$, and then two equilibrium values appear (figure: $a = 3.04$).

These two value are also calcurated by a steady equation like above one.
The equation is expressed by \[ x_n = x_{n+2} = ax_{n+1}(1-x_{n+1}) = a(ax_n (1-x_n))(1-(ax_n(1-x_n))). \] This is a cubic equation, being solved by 0, (a-1)/a ($= x_1^*$), and the two solutions: \[ x^*_{2\pm} = \frac{a+1 \pm \sqrt{(a+1)(a-3)}}{2a}. \] Therefore, $x_n$ is not stable in $x_1^*$ but in $x_{2\pm}^*$.
The branches of such equilibrium point repeat further branching as a becomes larger.


And finally, the countless equilibrium points allow the chaotic trajectory to vary greatly with small deviations from the initial value.
